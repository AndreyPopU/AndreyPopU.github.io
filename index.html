Protect the Restaurant


Protect the Restaurant is a 2D top-down game I developed for the GMTK 2020 game jam. The theme was “Out of Control”, and I took the approach of dealing with customers that are out of control.


The game consists of 4 levels where angry customers raid your restaurant in a wild fury, breaking all your furniture and scaring away other customers. The goal of the game is to move the furniture out of the way of the angry customer repeatedly, until their stamina runs out and they leave.

Development Challenges


Although the jam game doesn’t feature A* pathfinding, it was added consequently in a post-game jam version of the game. It was difficult to implement but not impossible. However, then I realized that moving furniture around doesn’t change the A* generated map, so I tried to implement D* pathfinding instead. However, it proved to be the hardest thing to do in the case of this project and at the time I had a main project that I was working on so I decided to leave it at that and move on.

What I learned

  • A* pathfinding
  • How parenting and children work
  • Cutscenes and dialogue

Sticky Shot


This is a game made for the GMTK 2021 jam where the theme was “Joined Together”. In this game you use a slingshot to shoot out a slimy ball that sticks to obstacles in it’s way. As you upgrade your slingshot the ball gets launched further, sweeps away more obstacles and gets bigger, until finally it goes past the city, which is the last obstacle of the game.

Development Challenges


This game is pretty simple and short, so the challenge was not in developing the game but organizing my workflow. I learned to use the MOSCOW method of development and prioritize what is important and what is unnecessary. Having a list of priorities really helped me to understand how to focus on the important features first and leave the rest for later. This ensured that even if I came short on time I would still have a game to upload at the end of the jam.

What I learned

  • Proper game loop
  • Layer masks, 2D sorting and Tilesets
  • Parallax
  • Work Under Stress
  • Use the MOSCOW Method

Knighty


Knighty started as a simple 2D platformer tutorial with basic movement and enemies and then evolved into a much bigger project. The final product features multiple types of enemies, collectables, weapons and game mechanics. This project is one of my biggest learning experiences in how to design and build a game, write code, keep organized structure in a project and ultimately release a finished product.

Development Challenges


As I mentioned before, Knighty turned out to be a pretty big project, therefore it became harder to keep it neat and tidy, and most importantly – free of bugs. Implementing new features and making sure they don’t disrupt dynamics between old ones progressively got harder as well. Since I was going all out, I decided that a Save&Load system which keeps track of the stage and all of it’s entities’ positions and stats is a must-have. However, it quickly proved rather difficult to accomplish such a task, though it all worked out in the end.

What I learned

  • Class Inheritance
  • Save&Load System
  • Keeping code simple, organized and optimized

Cold Wheels


Cold Wheels is a small project on which I worked with the goal of understanding physics in Unity better in mind. The game is a racing simulator type of game where you race against an AI controlled car. There are 4 different tracks on which you can race, first one to finish 3 laps wins.

Development Challenges


One of the greater challenges this project imposed was to simulate realistic car movement which is also affected by physics in Unity. To do that I had examine how car suspension and drifting works in real life and try to recreate it in my game.

What I learned

  • Deeper understanding of Unity’s physics engine
  • Various Unity components such as joint, spring, hinge joint, etc.
  • Simple AI Behaviour

Scoring Roll


This is a game made for the GMTK2022 game jam, where the theme was “Roll of the Dice”. By rolling the dice you step on tiles and activate their effects, which then, get multiplied by the number you rolled. I am proud to say that the game turned out to be in the top 500 games out of 3000 submissions.

Development Challenges


The hard part of this project wasn’t developing it but coming up with a unique idea for the game. As I mentioned before, the theme is “Roll of the Dice” and although that gives us a lot of creative freedom it is easy to fall into the trap of going with the first idea in mind and creating a very basic and ordinary game. I think this is what got me ahead of other games, along with the completed look of the game.

What I learned

  • Polishing Features
  • Progressive Difficulty
  • Time Management
  • The Power of Brainstorming

Blocky Wheels


This is my Highschool final project and probably one of my biggest projects. Blocky Wheels is a 1-4 player racing simulator featuring various cars & car customization, power ups, race events, campaign mode and much more. The race events consist of transporting players into incoming traffic or activating the nitro of every player for a duration of time. Campaign mode is a prebuilt set of levels which upon completion unlock new vehicles for the player to drive. The city is procedurally generated and therefore can be infinite. Car traffic followed road laws such as waiting for traffic lights to turn green, or stopping before a sidewalk in case a pedestrian is crossing the road. The vehicles are able to change lanes as well.


Unfortunately, the project slowly crumbled in my attempts to implement working multiplayer, part of the reason being that I made the mistake to start implementing it so late into development. In the end the project remained unfinished but even though I couldn’t publish the full game, it was a great learning experience and it deserves to be mentioned. Nevertheless, feel free to take a look at the GitHub page of the project (link below).

Development Challenges


The hardest challenge for this project was trying to understand how multiplayer in video games works and how to implement it into my game. As I mentioned before, I made the mistake of trying to implement multiplayer in the game way too late, without having prior experience with it beforehand. Being forced to test the game on 2 different devices and having the most unpredictable and inconsistent bugs slowly made me lose hope and ultimately give up.

What I learned

  • Steam API integration into the game
  • Basic knowledge of Networking, UDP and TCP Internet Protocols
  • Using Mirror Networking Library for Unity
